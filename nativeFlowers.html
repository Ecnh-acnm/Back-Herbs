Nyoongah Aboriginal Pit Healing Ceremony

The Nyoongah aboriginal people of south-west Australia hold the oldest living tradition of flower essence therapy (Barnao & Barnao, 1997), which involves the selection of bespoke flowers that are tailored to the individual. Flowers are harvested and soaked in a billabong, stream or spring overnight, and then placed over coals in a pit and drenched with water (ABFE, nd). The “patient” lies on top of the moistened flowers and is covered with kangaroo skins or paperbark sheets. Music accompanies this healing ceremony, including didgeridoo, clap sticks and chanting. The essence of the flowers permeates the individual and they enter into a deep space of healing.

Australian Essence History

Australia’s flowering plants are arguably the oldest on the planet: the first angiosperms are thought to have developed in the area occupied by Australia around 144 million years ago. Due to the separation of the continents from Gondwana, the geographical isolation of Australia meant that our flowers have adapted for enhanced survival and, as a result, exhibit unique characteristics. 50% of Australia’s native flowers exist nowhere else on the planet, and the diversity of plants native to the southwestern region of West Australia is greater than that of the Amazon (Barnao & Barnao, 1997).

Australian Flower Reverence

Plants use their flowers as an expression of their desire for evolution and transformation. Flowers are a representation of a plants healing pathway, or the pathway that is available for becoming more whole. Our attraction or repulsion to certain flowers should always be recognised and used as an insight into our own healing pathway at any point in time. These strong emotions (either positive or negative) are our response to the subtle manifestation of the flower’s energy and vibration frequency that resonates with where we are at (Barnao & Barnao,1997).
